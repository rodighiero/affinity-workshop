A four-day workshop on network design · Bolzano · July 6–9, 2026

Network design is the practice of turning collections of texts, people, or objects into visual networks — maps in which what matters is not individual items but the relationships between them.

A network shows entities as points and their connections as links, so that similarity, proximity, and grouping become something you can see and explore. In this workshop, network design is treated less as a way of decorating data than as a way of knowing it: the choices made while building a network — what counts as a connection, how the layout is arranged, what is placed at the center or the edge — shape what the final map lets you understand. Learning to design networks therefore means learning to read complex material critically, to make its structure visible, and to ask what each design decision reveals or hides.

The live network on this site is a worked example of what you will build during the week.

Learning outcomes

By the end of the workshop, students will be able to:

  • Apply the basics of text analysis to extract concepts and relationships from a single text;
  • Build and read network visualizations from a small corpus of documents;
  • Interpret a network critically, understanding how design choices shape what it reveals;
  • Situate their own work within the introductory scientific literature on network design;
  • Present their results clearly in a short final presentation.

Requirements

No prior knowledge of text analysis or network visualization is required; the workshop is designed to be accessible to participants from any background. Each student should come prepared with two things:

  • One long text — an article, essay, chapter, or interview — to work with during the close-reading sessions;
  • A small corpus of 20–30 short documents — such as paper abstracts — to turn into a network.

Ideally, both the text and the corpus should relate to your own research, so that the methods learned can be applied directly to your work.

Approach

The workshop combines theory, practice, and literacy. Each session opens with a short presentation that introduces a concept and situates it within the scientific literature, followed by hands-on work in which students apply the method to their own material. Rather than treating visualization as a finished product, the emphasis is on the decisions behind it — so that students leave not only able to build a network, but able to read one critically and explain what it shows. The week follows a deliberate progression, from close reading of a single text, to comparison across a small corpus, to the design and interpretation of a network, culminating in a short presentation of each student's results.

Expected outcomes

By the close of the week, each participant will have produced a network built from their own corpus and shared it with the group in a short final presentation. They leave with two things to take forward: a reusable workflow for moving from texts to a map, and a starting set of references for going further on their own. The closing discussion is an outcome in itself, turning the individual projects into a shared conversation across the participants' different fields.

Schedule

The workshop runs over four days, from July 6 to 9, with a morning session (10am–12pm) and an afternoon session (1pm–4pm) each day. Most sessions pair a short presentation with hands-on work, moving from the analysis of a single text toward the design of a network.

Teachers

Dario Rodighiero is a researcher in information design and the digital humanities at the University of Groningen, affiliated with metaLAB (at) Harvard/Berlin/Basel and the Berkman Klein Center for Internet & Society. His work focuses on network visualization as a way of knowing — designing maps of scholarly and cultural data that turn complex corpora into navigable, readable spaces. Across projects ranging from affinity mapping to large-scale physicalizations and AI-assisted annotation, he treats visualization not as display but as a research practice in its own right. His research has appeared in venues across information design and the digital humanities, and his projects — from mapping scholarly communities to printing networks at architectural scale — ask how visualization can extend what a single reader is able to perceive of a field too vast to hold at once.

Sabina Rosenbergová is Assistant Professor of Cultural Heritage at the University of Groningen (Campus Fryslân) and a Marie Curie Fellow. An art historian by training, she earned her PhD from Sapienza University of Rome and Masaryk University in Brno, with research spanning Italy, Czechia, France, and the United States, including fellowships at the Bibliotheca Hertziana (Max Planck Institute) in Rome and CUNY in New York. Her current MSCA project examines how objects and spaces are transformed as they become heritage — how museums, collections, and conservation remake the meaning of what they set out to preserve, and what is gained and lost in the passage. She brings to the workshop a deep grounding in cultural heritage, collections, and the interpretation of historical material — perspectives that connect closely to the close reading and meaning-making at the heart of text analysis.
