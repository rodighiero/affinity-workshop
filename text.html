The microscope to Network's telescope: drop in one document and read it in layers — size, readability, keywords, phrases, sentiment. Runs live in your browser; nothing is uploaded.

No text handy? — In Praise of Visual Representation. Loaded — click Analyze.

Measurements

How big the document is, and the shape of its sentences and words: compromise splits the text into word tokens and sentences (paragraphs by blank lines) and each is counted, then divided to give average sentence and word lengths. Reading time assumes a common silent-reading rate of ~200 words per minute.

Vocabulary

How varied and how densely packed the word choice is: lexical diversity is the type–token ratio of unique words to the total, and lexical density the share that are content words (nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs). The spectrum counts how many words appear once, twice, and so on — most occur only once, the long tail of rare words typical of natural language. Assumes English.

Grammar

The grammatical makeup of the prose: compromise tags each word's part of speech, so the bar shows each part of speech as a share of all words, the ratios contrast styles (nominal vs verbal, more or less descriptive), and sentences are split into active and passive voice. Assumes English.

Entities

The people, places and organizations the text names: compromise spots these named entities by their form and context, then lists the distinct ones of each kind (with a count when a name recurs). Detection is heuristic, so expect the odd miss or stray word. Assumes English.

Readability

How demanding the prose is to read: from sentence length, syllables per word, and the share of complex (3+ syllable) and long words, several formulas estimate a school grade. The Flesch reading-ease score (0–100) is plotted on the scale; the bars compare grade-level estimates (Flesch–Kincaid, Gunning fog, SMOG, Coleman–Liau), which rarely agree exactly. The syllable heuristic assumes English.

Sentiment

The emotional tone of the writing: each word is looked up in the AFINN lexicon, which rates words from −5 to +5, and the valences are summed (a preceding “not” or “never” flips the sign). Above zero leans positive, below negative. A blunt, lexicon-based estimate — it misses sarcasm and context. Assumes English.

Keywords

The content words the document leans on most, two ways: compromise keeps the nouns and verbs and reduces each to its base form (so “communities” and “community” count as one). One list ranks them by raw frequency (Luhn, 1958); the other by distinctiveness — how much more often a word appears here than in general English — so telling terms outrank merely common ones. Assumes English.

Recurring phrases

Recurring multi-word expressions, not just single words: compromise extracts noun phrases, split on stopwords into runs of two or more content words (kept if they occur at least twice). One list ranks them by how often they repeat; the other by how tightly their words bind — co-occurring far more than chance — so true terms outrank merely frequent pairs. Assumes English.

Paragraph by paragraph

The document's arc, read one paragraph at a time: each row tracks how long a paragraph is, how hard it is to read (Flesch reading-ease), its emotional tone (AFINN), and its most distinctive words — so you can see pacing, difficulty and mood shift across the text. Assumes English.
